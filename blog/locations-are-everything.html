With Text Data, Locations are Everything

Thomas Lillo · Aug 18, 2024

Location is one of the most critical components of any data analysis work, particularly when extracting geographic information from text. The context of where a geological or engineering event takes place is one of the most significant factors in how successful it will likely be. Before you can extract a location reliably, though, you need to understand the format it might be hiding in — and there are more of these formats than most people expect.

There are three primary coordinate systems you'll run into when pulling geographic information out of unstructured text.

Geographic Coordinate System

The Geographic Coordinate System is the one most people are already familiar with: it uses latitude, longitude, and altitude to specify a position on the Earth's surface. Paris, for example, sits at approximately 48.871728, 2.350468, with the Eiffel Tower reaching about 300 meters in elevation. It's a simple, universal way to describe a point — but simple doesn't mean it always shows up in text the same way twice.

Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM)

The Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) system divides the Earth into zones and expresses position with meter-based coordinates, which makes it well suited for precise, on-the-ground measurement. Tokyo, for instance, sits at approximately 35.6824° N, 139.7594° E, UTM 31U. That zone designation isn't a footnote — without it, the easting and northing values on their own are ambiguous and can't be mapped to an actual point on Earth.

Projected Coordinate Systems

Projected Coordinate Systems flatten the Earth's curved surface using a projection method such as Mercator or Lambert Conformal Conic. Denver, Colorado is a good example of this approach, sitting at around 39.7392° N, 104.9903° W on a Lambert projection. Different projections trade off accuracy in different ways — distance, area, or shape — so knowing which projection produced a coordinate matters just as much as the coordinate itself.

Understanding the formatting of each system, and the essential pieces of information required for each one to map to a spot on Earth, will help you build better algorithms for extracting this information from text.
